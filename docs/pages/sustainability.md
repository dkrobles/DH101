# Sustainability & Ethics

My Research:
My research focused on the environmental impact of AI systems like ChatGPT. I learned that AI has a significant but often invisible carbon footprint due to the energy used in training and everyday use.

Training a large model like GPT-3 is estimated to produce about 552 tons of CO2, comparable to hundreds of flights or over a decade of emissions from an average U.S. household. Even a single AI query (~4.55g CO2) seems small, but scales massively when used by millions of people.

Beyond carbon, AI also requires large amounts of water for cooling data centers and depends on mining rare earth materials for hardware. A key idea from Kate Crawford’s Atlas of AI is that AI is “cloud-based” in a symbolic sense– it is physical infrastructure with real environmental costs.

Personal Reflection:
My AI use habits: I use AI regularly for schoolwork, writing, coding help, and brainstorming. It has become part of my normal workflow, often used multiple times a day.

Estimated impact: Each query has a small carbon cost, but frequent use adds up over time. While still lower than major activities like driving or flying, AI use is not impact-free when scaled globally.

What I’m willing to change: I can be more intentional by reducing unnecessary prompts, combining questions into fewer queries, and using AI only when it meaningfully adds value.

What I can’t/won't change: I will continue using AI because it is essential for learning and productivity in my personal life. It is deeply integrated into academic and professional workflows, making full avoidance unrealistic.

Systemic vs. Individual:
What individuals can do:
- Use AI more intentionally
- Reduce unnecessary usage
- Support awareness of AI’s environmental impact

What requires systemic change
- Renewable-powered data centers
- More efficient AI models
- Transparent emissions reporting
- Regulation of energy and water use in tech infrastructure

Who is responsible?
Responsibility is shared, but not equal. Tech companies and governments have the greatest responsibility because they control infrastructure and policy. Users play a smaller role through demand and usage habits, but cannot solve the issue alone.
