Research Documentation:
I relied on a number of academic and institutional sources to conduct my research on the environmental impact of AI, particularly CO2 emissions during both the training process and usage of these algorithms. Perhaps the most cited statistic was the one obtained from studies dedicated to the large language models such as GPT-3, where it was established that only the training process generated roughly 552 tons of CO2 emissions. Moreover, I relied on statistics on emissions caused by a single use of the service ChatGPT, which estimated roughly 4.55 grams of CO2 emissions. 

To make sense of the seemingly arbitrary data, I related the environmental impact of AI emissions to other activities such as air travel, energy usage at home, or carbon dioxide uptake by the tree population.

One of the biggest challenges throughout the research process involved collecting reliable and current information. The fact is that AI firms are not consistent with releasing full environmental impact reports and also vary greatly when it comes to emissions based on the computational locations and types of power grids used. Moreover, estimations based on per-query calculations remain inconsistent as all of the computing infrastructure is shared among numerous services.

The other problem related to the current research is that it seems to be limited by the focus on training emissions since inference (daily queries from users) might become a larger part of total emissions for AI models.

However, generally speaking, it can be concluded that although individual queries from users remain relatively insignificant, when calculated in mass, they become quite substantial. The fact of the matter is that the overall uncertainty involved in measurement implies an even greater problem that remains hidden within the system.

Artist Statement:
The biggest environmental problem with AI is that its emissions are invisible to us. There is no tangible feel associated with AI usage because one does not physically drive or fly while engaging with it. Yet there is an entire physical process involving data centers, cooling, and power grids behind every interaction, resulting in the release of emissions.

What surprised me about AI is the huge gap between individual usage and the system–level training process. An interaction with ChatGPT creates a negligible amount of CO2–only a few grams at a time. However, scaling up to millions or even billions of queries will make their impact similar to those of industries and factories. 

AI itself is not intrinsically unsustainable, but its current development requires excessive energy consumption and opacity regarding its effects. To make AI more sustainable, businesses should use data centers powered by renewables, provide transparent information on carbon emissions, and strive for efficiency in the design of AI models, ensuring lower computational power to achieve comparable results.

In this situation, my role has two parts. Personally, I can be more mindful of when I use AI applications and determine if the need for their use arises. Yet, I am well aware of the limitations of my own efforts. The vast majority of negative environmental consequences result from the infrastructural decisions made by major technological firms. Therefore, system-level actions are inevitable.

Overall, this project has helped me grasp the idea that AI is not some abstract cloud. Instead, it is tangible, resource-intensive, and heavily dependent on the natural environment.
